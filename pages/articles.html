History

Underwater exploration evolved from human breath-hold diving to pressurized submersibles, sonar mapping, and today’s ROVs/AUVs with onboard computing. This timeline highlights the key milestones that progressively expanded our ability to reach, see, measure and work underwater.

Read it like a story: early engineering (bells, suits), then the big acceleration from wars and industry (sonar, power, integration), and finally the modern era of autonomy and global programs (full-seafloor mapping, Ocean Decade).

Big shifts to remember

A few moments where ocean exploration changes scale: not just “one invention”, but a new way to operate.

Systems leap

1914–1945: sensors + integration

Submarine warfare pushed rapid progress in acoustics (hydrophones/sonar), navigation, power and procedures. After WWII, these technologies quickly spread to science and industry.

From records to routine

1960–1964: Trieste → Alvin

Trieste proves full-depth access is possible. Alvin makes deep exploration repeatable: sampling, cameras, manipulators and mission workflows.

Robots take over depth

1970s–today: ROVs & AUVs

With ROVs, depth becomes a design constraint instead of a human limit. With AUVs, mapping becomes scalable: plan → survey → process → iterate.

Exploration at global scale

2017–2030: Seabed 2030 & the Ocean Decade

Modern exploration is also about coordination: shared data standards, open maps, and long-term observation. Robotics and automation make these programs realistic.
