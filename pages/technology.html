Deep Dive

Autonomous Underwater Vehicles (AUV)

AUVs are untethered robots that follow pre-programmed flight paths to map the seafloor without human intervention.

How it Works

Unlike ROVs, AUVs have no cable connecting them to the ship. They must carry their own power and “think” for themselves.

Navigation (The “Blind” Problem)

  • INS (Inertial Navigation): Uses gyroscopes/accelerometers to estimate position from a starting point 1
  • DVL (Doppler Velocity Log): Sonar that measures velocity relative to the seafloor (or water mass) to correct drift 1
  • Acoustic Comms: Sends small data packets (status updates) via sound waves 2
Figure 1: An AUV operating autonomously. (Credit: Exail)
Robotics

Remotely Operated Vehicles (ROV)

ROVs are the workhorses of the deep, physically connected to the ship by a long cable called a tether (or umbilical).

Figure 2: A SeaBotix ROV, designed for inspection and observation tasks.

The Tether Advantage

Because they are plugged into the ship, ROVs have two massive advantages over AUVs:

  • High/Continuous Power: Not limited by onboard batteries, enabling longer and heavier-duty operations.
  • Real-Time Control: The pilot sees live video and can react instantly.

Tooling & Instrumentation

Many scientific ROVs use manipulator arms to collect samples (rock, sediment, biological specimens) and place them into baskets or payload areas 3

Human Element

Crewed Submersibles

Nothing replaces the human eye. Vehicles like Alvin and Limiting Factor take scientists to the very bottom.

Engineering for Pressure

Pressure increases quickly with depth: roughly +1 atmosphere every 10 meters, reaching over 1,000 atmospheres in the deepest trenches 4

  • Pressure Hull: Strong shapes (often spheres) and high-strength materials protect the crew.
  • Syntactic Foam: Buoyancy material that withstands crushing pressure at depth.
  • Life Support: Systems that maintain breathable air and remove CO₂.
