1. Early Experiments and Pioneers (1825–1898)

In the 19th century, engineers experimented with heavy diving suits and the first submarines. Designs like Le Plongeur in France or the Holland VI in the United States proved that underwater navigation and propulsion were possible, even if range and safety were still very limited.

2. Bathysphere and the Birth of Modern Scuba (1930s–1940s)

In 1934, William Beebe and Otis Barton used the Bathysphere, a steel sphere lowered by cable, to reach 923 m, a depth never seen before by humans. Less than ten years later, Jacques-Yves Cousteau and Émile Gagnan created the Aqua-Lung, the first widely used open-circuit scuba system, allowing divers to move freely without a cable.

3. Trieste, Alvin and Scientific Exploration (1960–1977)

On 23 January 1960, the bathyscaphe Trieste reached the bottom of the Mariana Trench at about 10,916 m, with Jacques Piccard and Don Walsh on board. A few years later, the submersible Alvin entered service. It would become one of the most important research subs, famously exploring hydrothermal vents and later the wreck of the Titanic.

4. ROVs and AUVs: Robots Take Over (1980–2000)

From the 1980s, remotely operated vehicles (ROVs) became standard tools for industry and science. In 1985, ROVs and towed cameras helped locate the Titanic. At the same time, autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs) such as REMUS and Autosub were developed, capable of mapping large areas without a tether.

5. Record-Breaking Deep Dives (2010s)

In 2012, filmmaker James Cameron piloted the Deepsea Challenger to the Mariana Trench in the deepest solo dive ever achieved. Later, the submersible Limiting Factor completed multiple dives to the deepest points of several oceans, proving that repeated access to the hadal zone is possible.

6. Future Projects and Ocean Worlds (2030 and Beyond)

Future ocean exploration focuses on full mapping of the seafloor (Seabed 2030), long-endurance AUVs powered by advanced batteries, and permanent underwater habitats for scientists. Technologies developed for Earth’s oceans will also be used to prepare missions to icy moons such as Europa, where liquid oceans may hide conditions favourable to life.
